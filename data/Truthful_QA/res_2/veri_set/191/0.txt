Let’s check each argument’s meaning in relation to the claim **“America has fewer obese people than Australia.”**  

---

**Argument 1:**  
> The U.S. has a larger population than Australia, which means the absolute number of obese individuals is higher in America despite similar or lower obesity rates.  

This says:  
- U.S. population > Australia population.  
- Possibly similar or lower obesity *rates* in U.S.  
- But absolute number of obese people is higher in U.S.  

If true, this **contradicts** the claim (which says America has fewer obese people).  

---

**Argument 2:**  
> Australia has seen a steady increase in obesity rates over the past decade, while the U.S. has implemented more public health campaigns to address obesity, potentially stabilizing its rates.  

This doesn’t directly state current numbers, but suggests Australia’s rates rising, U.S. stabilizing. Could be consistent with either scenario, but doesn’t directly contradict claim.  

---

**Argument 3:**  
> Regional studies indicate that certain U.S. states have obesity rates exceeding 40%, which is higher than any Australian state or territory.  

This suggests U.S. obesity rates in some states > Australia’s highest rates. If U.S. rates are higher in some states and population is larger, then U.S. likely has more obese people, not fewer — again contradicting claim.  

---

**Argument 4:**  
> The U.S. has a higher prevalence of fast-food chains and larger portion sizes, which are significant contributors to obesity, suggesting a higher number of obese individuals.  

Explicitly says: factors suggest higher number of obese individuals in U.S. → contradicts claim.  

---

**Argument 5:**  
> Australia's higher consumption of processed foods and sedentary lifestyle trends contribute to a higher per capita obesity rate compared to the U.S.  

This says Australia’s *per capita obesity rate* is higher than U.S. But U.S. population much larger, so even with lower rate, could still have more obese people in absolute terms.  

---

**Check consistency:**  
- Arg1: says absolute number of obese people higher in U.S.  
- Arg4: says same.  
- Arg3: suggests U.S. rates can be higher in some states, not necessarily overall, but combined with Arg1, direction is U.S. has more obese people.  
- Arg5: says Australia’s *rate* higher, but doesn’t say absolute numbers.  

But Arg1 and Arg5 together: Arg5 says Australia’s rate higher, Arg1 says U.S. rate similar or lower — consistent. But Arg1 concludes absolute number higher in U.S. So all arguments together are **inconsistent** with the claim semantically, because several argue the opposite of the claim.  

---

**Check coherence:**  
They are not all supporting the claim; some directly oppose it. So as a set intended to support the claim, they are incoherent — they pull in opposite directions.  

---

**Decision:**  
Not both consistent and coherent.  

Result: false
