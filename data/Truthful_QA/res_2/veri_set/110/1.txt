First, let's check **consistency**:

- Argument 1 says labor laws have prevented significant increases in working hours.
- Argument 2 says technology has made employees more accessible, contributing to longer perceived workdays.
- Argument 3 says data shows average weekly hours have remained relatively stable.
- Argument 4 says decline of unionization has led to fewer protections against excessive hours.

These can all be true together:  
Labor laws may have historically limited hours, but union decline may have weakened protections recently.  
Technology may extend perceived workday without necessarily increasing official weekly hours.  
Official data may show stability even if perceived hours or accessibility have increased.  
No direct contradiction in factual claims.

Now check **coherence**:

The claim is: "The working hours of Americans have increased over time."

Arguments collectively:
- Arg1: Labor laws prevented significant increases (suggests no increase due to regulation).
- Arg3: Data shows hours stable (suggests no increase).
- Arg2: Technology increased perceived workday (could support increase in "effective" hours).
- Arg4: Union decline reduced protections against excessive hours (could support increase in recent times).

But there’s a tension: Arg1 and Arg3 suggest no increase, Arg2 and Arg4 suggest pressures or perceptions that could lead to increase.  
However, they could together explain why hours haven’t increased much despite pressures — but that would **contradict** the claim rather than support it coherently.

For the set to be coherent in *supporting* the claim, they should align in direction. Here, Arg1 and Arg3 directly undermine the claim.  
Thus, as a set supporting the claim, they are **incoherent** because they are not unified in supporting the claim’s truth — some argue against it.

Since coherence fails, the set is not both consistent and coherent.

Result: false
